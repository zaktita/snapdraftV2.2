Most content teams already plan in a spreadsheet. Titles in one column, copy in another, formats and dates alongside. Then the plan leaves the spreadsheet and enters the slow zone: briefs, design queues, revision rounds, exports.

What if the spreadsheet *was* the brief?

## The one-row-one-visual principle

A batch workflow treats each spreadsheet row as a complete unit of work:

- **Title** - the headline or hook of the visual
- **Description** - what the image should communicate
- **Format** - square for feed, portrait for stories, wide for banners

That's enough information to produce a finished visual, provided the system already knows what your brand looks like. This is the piece most AI tools miss: they make you re-describe your brand in every prompt, and every re-description drifts a little.

## Why batching changes the economics

Generating one image at a time keeps you in the loop for every single asset. Batching inverts the ratio: you spend your time on the plan (the spreadsheet) and on the review (the results), not on the production in between.

A 50-row calendar that would take a design queue two weeks becomes an upload, a coffee, and a review session. And because every row is generated from the same brand profile, the review is fast - you are checking details, not rescuing off-brand drafts.

## Regeneration is part of the workflow

No batch is perfect on the first pass. The important thing is that fixing row 37 shouldn't mean touching rows 1 through 36. Row-level regeneration - with the same brand profile, same format, same inputs - keeps iteration cheap and isolated.

## Try it on your real calendar

The best test isn't a demo dataset; it's next month's actual content plan. Export it as CSV, add a format column, and see how much of your production pipeline collapses into a single upload.
