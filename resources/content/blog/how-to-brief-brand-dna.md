**TL;DR:** Brand DNA in SnapDraft only works as well as the references you feed it. Use 5-10 approved posts that show the steady-state brand look. Skip experiments, competitor moodboards, and one-off campaign neon.

Brand DNA only works as well as the references you feed it. Random screenshots produce random constraints. A tight set of approved assets produces a profile your calendar can trust.

## What good references mean

Aim for **5-10 images** that already represent how the brand should look in the channel you publish:

- Recent posts that leadership or the client still likes
- Campaign stills that match current palette and lighting
- Guideline exports when they show real layout habits. Not just a logo on white.

Skip moodboards full of competitors, stock you would never post, or one experimental reel that broke the grid on purpose.

## Teach the durable brand, not the exception

If last month's launch used neon for a week, do not let that dominate the reference set unless neon is the new normal. Brand DNA should encode the **steady state** visual rules: palette, composition habits, typographic mood.

When the brand evolves, update references deliberately. That is cheaper than rewriting prompts on every row.

## Connect DNA to the calendar

Once the profile is locked, your spreadsheet becomes the brief. Titles, descriptions, and formats inherit the same look. That is how [on-brand consistency](/glossary/on-brand-consistency) scales across a week without a designer ticket per post.

## Try it on one client first

Pick a brand with a clear visual history. Build Brand DNA in SnapDraft, generate ten rows from a real sheet, and review for drift. If outliers appear, fix them in the [Canvas Editor](/glossary/canvas-editor). Then decide whether the references need a refresh.
